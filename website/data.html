Chapter 02

The Data

What 683 City of Los Angeles civil service job bulletins — and their companion applicant composition dataset — can, and cannot, tell us about who gets in.

About the source

An administrative corpus, read as text.

Our project works with several hundred civil service job bulletins issued by the City of Los Angeles Personnel Department, along with a companion dataset on applicant demographics. Each bulletin is a plain-text document focused on one job classification. It includes the job title, salary range, description of duties, required education or experience, selection process, application procedures, and important deadlines.

The companion Job Applicant Composition dataset provides aggregated demographic information by job title, including categories such as gender and race/ethnicity. Taken together, these datasets help us study how the City of Los Angeles officially defines public employment and how different groups of applicants are distributed across job categories. They cannot fully reveal the lived experience of work, the internal decision-making of hiring, or the deeper social inequalities that shape who becomes "qualified" in the first place.

The data was generated from official job bulletins produced through the City's classification and hiring systems. These bulletins are not neutral descriptions of work. They are administrative and legal documents that define the terms of public examination and determine who is eligible to apply for civil service positions. The dataset was assembled and released in 2019 through a collaboration between the City of Los Angeles and Kaggle as part of the Data Science for Good program.

Scope

What's in, what's out.

In the corpus

  • — Job title and class code
  • — Annual salary range or flat rate
  • — Description of duties
  • — Stated minimum education requirement
  • — Required years of work experience
  • — Required licenses / certifications
  • — Exam type (open-competitive, promotional, or both)
  • — Application procedure and important deadlines
  • — Aggregated applicant gender and race/ethnicity by job title (companion dataset)

Not in the corpus

  • — Individual applicant records or résumés
  • — Exam scores or interview outcomes
  • — Who was actually hired or retained
  • — Internal promotion histories of named employees
  • — Informal networks and recommendations
  • — Exempt, temporary, or emergency positions
  • — Bulletin revision history over time
  • — Applicant motivations and reasons for rejection

Critique

What this corpus can — and cannot — see.

The dataset can illuminate several important aspects of public-sector hiring. Because every civil service opening produces a bulletin, it gives a relatively comprehensive view of how the City formally organizes its workforce at a particular moment. It shows how different types of labor are categorized, named, and valued through salary ranges, duties, and qualification requirements. It also reveals patterns in the language of government employment — for example, whether job bulletins use technical or accessible language, and whether certain requirements might discourage applicants who do not already understand the civil service system.

At the same time, the dataset has major limitations. The bulletins describe job classifications rather than specific positions; a single classification may apply to many roles across different departments. The dataset cannot show what any individual worker actually does, where they work, how much autonomy they have, or what their workplace conditions are like. It also does not include hiring outcomes: the companion dataset shows who applied, but not who passed the exam, who was interviewed, who was hired, or who stayed in the job over time.

Many forms of information that would be necessary for a fuller understanding of city employment are absent — interview scores, applicant motivations, reasons for rejection, informal networks, internal recommendations, and the experiences of applicants navigating the hiring process. The dataset also leaves out workers who do not fit the civil service structure: exempt roles (including many senior or political positions) and temporary and emergency positions. As a result, the dataset gives a strong view of formal civil service hiring, but a weaker view of the entire public workforce.

The ontology of the dataset also has ideological effects. By organizing employment into categories such as job title, duties, salary, qualifications, and deadlines, the dataset presents public employment as orderly, standardized, and merit-based. This reflects the values of the civil service system, but it can also make the hiring process appear more neutral than it is. The dataset does not show how job requirements were created, how salary levels were negotiated, or how definitions of merit may reflect existing inequalities in education, professional experience, licensing, and access to institutional knowledge.

We read this dataset as a record of institutional self-description — not a complete picture of public employment itself.

Questions we couldn't answer

Five research questions excluded by the data.

Several proposed research questions cannot be answered with the available dataset and are explicitly excluded from our analysis. We list them here to make the boundary of what the bulletins can support visible.

  • Approaching-retirement demographics

    No age or hire-date data in the bulletins.

  • English-only publishing patterns

    All bulletins are in English; no contrast group exists to visualize.

  • Demographic makeup of the applicant pool at the individual level

    No applicant records are included; only aggregated counts by job title.

  • Informal knowledge networks

    No neighborhood or social-network data.

  • Criminal records and experience gaps

    No applicant history data.

Pipeline

Six steps from raw text to structured table.

01

Salary extraction via regex

Parsed annual salary ranges from free-text bulletin headers. Handled flat salaries, multiple bands, and missing values. Final salary coverage: 680 of 683 bulletins.

02

Education tier classification

Mapped phrases like "graduation from a recognized college" or "bachelor's degree in engineering" to five ordered tiers: No Formal Requirement → High School → Some College → Bachelor's → Graduate.

03

Experience parsing

Extracted required years of work experience using a layered regex matching constructions like "four years of full-time paid professional experience."

04

Exam access type parsing

Identified whether each bulletin advertised an open-competitive exam (any applicant), a promotional exam (current City employees only), or both.

05

Sector keyword classification

Assigned each title to one of five sectors — Public Safety, Technical/Engineering, Trades/Infrastructure, Administrative, Health/Social Services — using a curated keyword dictionary, hand-checked for ambiguous overlaps.

06

Reference file cleanup

Stripped boilerplate (filing instructions, ADA notices, application links) so downstream analysis runs on substantive bulletin text only.
