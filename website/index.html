Who gets to work

The opening question

Open a City of Los Angeles job application and you hit a wall before you reach the work itself: a bachelor's degree here, four years of specialized experience there, a promotional exam open only to people already inside. Long before the city decides who is qualified, the bulletin has decided who is allowed to ask.

Thesis

Credential barriers and internal mobility structures in the LA civil service create differential pathways to public sector employment — and formal education requirements do not consistently predict higher salaries.

Linn Khant Thuya & Song Chen

The bulletins ask us four things back.

Each question is paired with one visualization in the Narrative section and read against the limits of what bulletins can tell us.

01

QUESTION 01

Which sectors does the city pay most — and what does that valuation reveal?

We compare average salary midpoints across public safety, administrative, technical/engineering, trades, and health/social services to see how the City of LA values different kinds of labor.

02

QUESTION 02

Do higher education requirements actually predict higher salaries?

We test whether each step up the education ladder corresponds to a measurable salary premium — or whether credential requirements function as access barriers rather than markers of valued expertise.

03

QUESTION 03

How does required work experience translate into salary across sectors?

We map minimum years of experience against salary midpoints, color-coded by sector, to see whether accumulated experience reliably converts into compensation — or whether structural factors disrupt that relationship.

04

QUESTION 04

What does the promotional vs. open-competitive split reveal about who the system rewards?

We compare salary distributions for internally-restricted (promotional) versus open-competitive exams, by sector, to surface internal mobility advantages and the two-tier nature of civil service access.
