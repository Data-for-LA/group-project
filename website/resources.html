Chapter 04

Resources

Primary source, annotated bibliography, tools, and the citation we ask you to use when you build on our work.

Primary source

The dataset.

  • City of Los Angeles Personnel Department. Data Science for Good: City of Los Angeles. Kaggle, 2019.

    Approximately 683 plain-text civil service job bulletins published by the City of Los Angeles Personnel Department, together with a companion Job Applicant Composition dataset of aggregated gender and race/ethnicity counts by job title. The corpus is the entire empirical basis of this project.

Annotated bibliography

Credential requirements, examination tracks, and compensation in the City of Los Angeles civil service system.

Group members: Katie Lew, Hana Chloe Yoon, Song Chen, Jiayi Su, Linn Khant Thuya, Khant Nyi Hlaing.

  • Bolukbasi, Tolga, et al. "Man Is to Computer Programmer as Woman Is to Homemaker? Debiasing Word Embeddings." Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems 29 (NIPS 2016), 2016, pp. 4349–4357.

    Bolukbasi and colleagues demonstrate that word embeddings trained on large text corpora encode gender stereotypes, producing associations such as "man : computer programmer :: woman : homemaker." The authors argue that these patterns are inherited from biases already present in language data and develop methods for reducing gender bias in embeddings while preserving overall semantic usefulness. This source provides a technical background for understanding how computational language analysis can reproduce social stereotypes embedded in text, and helps contextualize later studies on gendered and racialized language in job advertisements. It focuses primarily on the computational properties of language models rather than applicant behavior, hiring outcomes, or public sector employment systems.

  • Brewer, Gene A., et al. "The Importance of Merit Principles for Civil Service Systems: Evidence from the U.S. Federal Sector." Review of Public Personnel Administration, vol. 43, no. 3, 2023, pp. 491–514. doi:10.1177/0734371X211026008.

    Brewer and colleagues examine how federal employees perceive merit principles within the U.S. civil service system, including open competition, qualification-based hiring, and protection from political influence. Workers who perceive stronger adherence to merit principles report higher job satisfaction, lower turnover intentions, and more positive perceptions of workplace quality. This helps situate credential requirements and examination systems within the broader logic of merit-based civil service governance, and provides important public sector context that differs from studies focused primarily on private sector hiring. A limitation is that the study focuses on perceptions within the federal workforce rather than municipal systems such as the City of Los Angeles.

  • Burn, Ian, et al. "Does Ageist Language in Job Ads Predict Age Discrimination in Hiring?" Journal of Labor Economics, vol. 40, no. 3, 2022, pp. 613–667. doi:10.1086/717730.

    Burn and colleagues examine whether youth-coded language in job advertisements predicts discriminatory hiring outcomes for older applicants. Combining computational text analysis with a large-scale audit study, they find that advertisements containing language associated with younger workers are linked to lower callback rates for older applicants. Methodologically valuable for our project because it models a rigorous approach to analyzing textual features in job bulletins and connecting those features to broader patterns of inequality. The study focuses on private-sector hiring and callback behavior rather than highly formalized public-sector systems governed by examinations and classification rules.

  • Carpenter, Jacqueline, et al. "Public Service Motivation as a Predictor of Attraction to the Public Sector." Journal of Vocational Behavior, vol. 80, no. 2, 2012, pp. 509–523. doi:10.1016/j.jvb.2011.08.004.

    This study examines how public service motivation (PSM) — a desire to contribute to society and the public good — influences attraction to government employment. Individuals with higher PSM are more likely to prefer public sector jobs, even when accounting for salary and job security. Useful for our project because it helps explain why applicants may be drawn to civil service positions for reasons beyond compensation alone, and provides context for understanding how public sector recruitment differs from private sector hiring. A limitation is that the study focuses on individual motivation rather than the institutional structure of civil service hiring systems.

  • Castilla, Emilio J., and Hye Jin Rho. "The Gendering of Job Postings in the Online Recruitment Process." Management Science, vol. 69, no. 11, 2023, pp. 6912–6939. doi:10.1287/mnsc.2023.4674.

    Castilla and Rho analyze a large dataset of online job postings to examine how gender-typed language varies across industries, occupations, and salary levels. Masculine-coded language appears more frequently in higher-paying and senior positions; feminine-coded language is more common in lower-paying occupations. Highly relevant to our project because it provides a large-scale empirical framework for analyzing how language in job bulletins may correspond to differences in status, compensation, and occupational segregation — especially useful for thinking about how gendered categories within the City of LA civil service (public safety vs. administrative) carry different linguistic expectations alongside different salary structures. Focuses on private-sector online recruitment rather than public-sector civil service systems.

  • Fabris, Alessandro, et al. "Fairness and Bias in Algorithmic Hiring: A Multidisciplinary Survey." ACM Transactions on Intelligent Systems and Technology, 2024. doi:10.1145/3696457.

    Fabris and colleagues survey interdisciplinary research on algorithmic hiring systems, including automated résumé screening, candidate ranking, and job-matching technologies. The paper examines how these systems can reproduce biases related to gender, race, age, and other demographic characteristics through both training data and model design. Useful for understanding how computational approaches to hiring and recruitment may embed social assumptions and inequalities, and for situating text-based analyses of job postings within larger debates about fairness in employment systems. Focuses primarily on automated hiring technologies in private-sector recruitment.

  • Gaucher, Danielle, et al. "Evidence That Gendered Wording in Job Advertisements Exists and Sustains Gender Inequality." Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, vol. 101, no. 1, 2011, pp. 109–128. doi:10.1037/a0022530.

    In this influential study, Gaucher and colleagues combine textual analysis and laboratory experiments to show that job advertisements in male-dominated occupations are more likely to contain masculine-coded language such as "competitive," "dominant," and "leader." This type of wording lowers women's anticipated sense of belonging and reduces their interest in applying, even when qualifications remain constant. Foundational to research on gendered hiring language and especially relevant to our project because many City of LA civil service bulletins — particularly in public safety or physically demanding classifications — may contain similar language. Focuses on private-sector job advertisements and relies on smaller-scale textual analysis compared to more recent computational approaches.

  • Heath, Amanda J., et al. "What Adds to Job Ads? The Impact of Equality and Diversity Information on Organizational Attraction in Minority and Majority Ethnic Groups." Journal of Occupational and Organizational Psychology, vol. 96, no. 4, 2023, pp. 872–896. doi:10.1111/joop.12454.

    Heath and colleagues examine how equality and diversity statements in job advertisements influence organizational attraction among minority and majority ethnic groups. Diversity-related messaging can increase organizational attraction among some minority applicants, but its effectiveness depends on organizational reputation and perceived credibility. Relevant to our project because many LA civil service bulletins include EEO language and diversity statements, particularly in departments concerned with demographic representation and public accountability. Relies on participant responses to hypothetical job advertisements rather than real-world application behavior.

  • Hu, Yang, et al. "Language in Job Advertisements and the Reproduction of Labor Force Gender and Racial Segregation." PNAS Nexus, vol. 3, no. 12, 2024, article pgae526. doi:10.1093/pnasnexus/pgae526.

    Hu and colleagues analyze a large corpus of job advertisements using computational text analysis to examine how occupational language reflects patterns of gender and racial segregation in the labor force. Job advertisements contain linguistic signals associated with demographic expectations and occupational identity. Highly relevant to our project because it provides a large-scale empirical framework for analyzing how language in LA civil service bulletins may similarly correspond to differences in occupational status, demographic composition, and hiring expectations. Extends earlier work by Gaucher et al. and Castilla and Rho by incorporating racialized language patterns. Focuses on broad occupational categories across the labor market rather than highly formalized public-sector systems.

  • Kobayashi, Vladimer B., et al. "Text Mining in Organizational Research." Organizational Research Methods, vol. 21, no. 3, 2018, pp. 733–765. doi:10.1177/1094428117722619.

    Kobayashi and colleagues provide a broad methodological overview of text mining techniques used in organizational research, including dictionary-based content analysis, machine learning, topic modeling, and other computational approaches to analyzing textual data. Offers methodological guidance for analyzing large collections of civil service job bulletins and helps justify the use of computational text analysis within an organizational research context. A methodological review rather than an empirical study, so it does not directly address the content or policy implications of civil service hiring systems.

  • Leibbrandt, Andreas, and John A. List. "Do Equal Employment Opportunity Statements Backfire? Evidence from a Natural Field Experiment on Job-Entry Decisions." NBER Working Paper Series, no. 25035, National Bureau of Economic Research, 2018. doi:10.3386/w25035.

    Leibbrandt and List use a natural field experiment to examine whether Equal Employment Opportunity statements in job advertisements affect application behavior. EEO language can influence applicant self-selection in unexpected ways, particularly in highly competitive job contexts, complicating the assumption that diversity statements always increase applicant diversity. Especially useful for thinking about how applicants may interpret institutional signals embedded in job postings rather than responding only to formal qualifications or salary information. Focuses on private-sector hiring contexts; conclusions should be interpreted cautiously as the paper remains a working paper.

  • Linos, Elizabeth. "More Than Public Service: A Field Experiment on Job Advertisements and Diversity in the Police." Journal of Public Administration Research and Theory, vol. 28, no. 1, 2018, pp. 67–85. doi:10.1093/jopart/mux032.

    Linos conducts a field experiment examining how different framings of police job advertisements affect the diversity of applicant pools. Advertisements emphasizing community service, collaboration, and helping the public attract significantly more applications from women and minority candidates than traditional law-enforcement-oriented messaging. One of the most relevant sources for our project because it directly studies language in public-sector recruitment — especially useful for interpreting LAPD job bulletins. Focuses specifically on police recruitment and a single type of advertisement framing, which may limit how broadly its findings apply across other civil service occupations.

  • Mahmoud, Ali B., et al. "Efficient Recruitment with Effective Job Advertisement: An Exploratory Literature Review and Research Agenda." International Journal of Organizational Analysis, vol. 30, no. 7, 2022, pp. 1–24. doi:10.1108/IJOA-09-2020-2421.

    Mahmoud and colleagues review existing research on job advertisements as recruitment and organizational communication tools. The paper identifies factors that influence applicant attraction including job requirement descriptions, compensation information, organizational image, and advertisement framing. Useful for understanding how civil service bulletins function not only as informational documents but also as recruitment tools designed to attract particular applicants. A literature review rather than an empirical study; its discussion of public-sector civil service systems is limited compared to its broader focus on recruitment practices across industries.

  • Salwender, Mona, and Dagmar Stahlberg. "Do Women Only Apply When They Are 100% Qualified, Whereas Men Already Apply When They Are 60% Qualified?" European Journal of Social Psychology, vol. 54, no. 7, 2024, pp. 1545–1557. doi:10.1002/ejsp.3109.

    Salwender and Stahlberg examine the widely circulated claim that women apply for jobs only when they meet all listed qualifications, whereas men apply when they meet only part of them. Using experimental designs, they find that the relationship between qualifications and application intentions is more complex than the popular "60% rule" suggests. Especially relevant to our project because it helps explain how formal credential and qualification requirements in job bulletins may shape applicant self-selection beyond their literal screening function — in the LA civil service context, educational requirements may operate as psychological thresholds that influence who feels encouraged to apply.

  • Senger, Elena, et al. "Deep Learning–Based Computational Job Market Analysis: A Survey on Skill Extraction and Classification from Job Postings." Proceedings of the First Workshop on Natural Language Processing for Human Resources (NLP4HR 2024), Association for Computational Linguistics, 2024, pp. 1–15.

    Senger and colleagues review recent deep learning approaches for extracting and classifying skills, qualifications, and job requirements from large collections of job postings. Provides technical background on how computational methods can be used to analyze qualification language and hiring criteria at scale, complementing Kobayashi et al. by focusing specifically on job posting analysis. Primarily a technical methods survey rather than a substantive study of hiring inequality or public-sector employment.

  • Sievert, Martin, et al. "From Wording to Workforce: Gendered Language in Public Job Advertisements Shapes Gender Diversity in Applicant Pools." Public Administration Review, vol. 86, no. 1, 2026, pp. 258–275. doi:10.1111/puar.13965.

    Sievert and colleagues analyze public-sector job advertisements to examine how gendered language relates to the gender composition of applicant pools. More feminine wording in public job advertisements is associated with a higher number and share of female applicants. Especially relevant to our project because it focuses specifically on public-sector recruitment and connects job bulletin language to measurable application patterns within bureaucratic hiring systems similar to the LA civil service. Builds on Gaucher et al. and Castilla and Rho by examining gendered language within a government employment context.

  • Uggerslev, Krista L., et al. "Recruiting Through the Stages: A Meta-Analytic Test of Predictors of Applicant Attraction at Different Stages of the Recruiting Process." Personnel Psychology, vol. 65, no. 3, 2012, pp. 597–660. doi:10.1111/j.1744-6570.2012.01254.x.

    Uggerslev and colleagues conduct a large meta-analysis examining the factors that influence applicant attraction throughout different stages of the recruitment process. They identify compensation, organizational image, job characteristics, and recruitment practices as key predictors of applicant attraction. Useful for understanding how applicants respond to information presented in job advertisements and helps contextualize the idea that salary information, qualification requirements, and organizational messaging in civil service bulletins may influence applicant self-selection behavior. Focuses largely on private-sector recruitment research and predates many recent developments in online hiring platforms.

  • Vogel, Dominik, et al. "Motivational Signals in Public Sector Job Advertisements and How They Relate to Attracting and Hiring Candidates." Public Management Review, vol. 26, no. 10, 2024, pp. 2868–2900. doi:10.1080/14719037.2023.2291068.

    Vogel and colleagues examine how motivational signals in public-sector job advertisements — public service mission, job security, career advancement — influence applicant attraction and recruitment outcomes. Advertisements emphasizing public service values are particularly effective in attracting candidates with stronger public service motivation. Relevant to our project because it shows how language in civil service bulletins can shape applicant behavior beyond formal qualification requirements alone. Complements Carpenter et al. by focusing not only on individual motivation but also on how institutional messaging affects recruitment patterns in public employment systems.
